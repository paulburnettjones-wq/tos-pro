DuckDuckGo Terms of Service Analysis

Data Usage & Privacy Good ▶

DuckDuckGo's ToS explicitly links to their Privacy Policy and states, "We don't track you." This is a positive aspect, indicating a commitment to user privacy. The ToS emphasizes the use of the services is governed by their privacy policy, which is a good sign for users concerned about data collection.

Acceptable Use & Termination Concerning ▶

DuckDuckGo reserves the right to suspend or terminate access to services if users violate the Terms or the Acceptable Use Policy. While this is standard, the breadth of the Acceptable Use Policy could be a concern if it's vaguely defined or subject to arbitrary interpretation. Users should review the Acceptable Use Policy carefully.

Feedback License Bad ▶

The clause regarding user feedback grants DuckDuckGo a "forever license" to use any feedback provided to improve their services without any payment or credit. This is a broad license that could be seen as exploitative, especially if the feedback leads to significant improvements or new features. Users should be aware that any suggestions they provide become the property of DuckDuckGo.

Third-Party Content Concerning ▶

DuckDuckGo disclaims responsibility for content from third parties, including search results and Instant Answers. While this is a common disclaimer, it means users are responsible for evaluating the accuracy and safety of content accessed through DuckDuckGo's services. The ToS explicitly states they cannot guarantee the accuracy or completeness of third-party content.

Warranty Disclaimer Bad ▶

DuckDuckGo disclaims all warranties, express or implied, including merchantability, fitness for a particular purpose, and non-infringement. Services are provided "as is" and "as available" with no guarantee of uninterrupted, secure, or error-free operation. This is a standard but broad disclaimer that limits DuckDuckGo's responsibility for the quality and reliability of its services. This is a significant limitation of liability for users.

Limitation of Liability Bad ▶

DuckDuckGo limits its liability for damages, including indirect, consequential, special, incidental, punitive, or exemplary damages, even if they have been advised of the possibility of such damages. The maximum aggregate liability is limited to the amount paid by the user for the services in the prior 12 months or $100 if no payment was made. This severely limits the potential compensation a user can receive for damages resulting from the use of DuckDuckGo's services.

Force Majeure Concerning ▶

DuckDuckGo is not responsible for events outside of their control (force majeure) that interrupt their ability to provide services. While this is a standard clause, it means users may experience disruptions in service without recourse if the interruption is caused by events like natural disasters or government actions.

Governing Law & Jurisdiction Concerning ▶

Any claim associated with DuckDuckGo's services or arising out of the Terms must be filed in the state or federal courts in New York, New York, USA, and New York law will govern. This means users outside of New York must travel to New York to resolve disputes and will be subject to New York law, which may be less favorable than their local laws. This can be a significant inconvenience and expense for users.

Changes to Terms Concerning ▶

DuckDuckGo will notify users of changes to the Terms by posting the modified version on their website. Continuing to access or use the services after any changes constitutes consent and agreement to the new terms. While notification is provided, users are responsible for reviewing the updated Terms, and continued use implies acceptance, even if the user is unaware of the changes. Users should regularly check for updates to the Terms.

DuckDuckGo's Terms of Service reflect a generally privacy-conscious approach, aligning with their brand image. The explicit statement of "We don't track you" and the link to their Privacy Policy are positive indicators. However, users should be aware of the standard disclaimers of warranties and limitations of liability, which are common in the industry but significantly limit DuckDuckGo's responsibility. The broad license granted for user feedback is also a point of concern.

While the ToS includes a notification clause for changes, continued use implies consent, placing the onus on the user to stay informed. The governing law and jurisdiction clause requiring disputes to be resolved in New York could be inconvenient for users outside of that region.

Overall, DuckDuckGo's ToS is better than many in terms of privacy focus, but users should carefully consider the limitations of liability and the feedback license before using the services.
